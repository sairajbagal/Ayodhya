City of immemorial antiquity

Ayodhya, the capital of the ancient Kosala kingdom, finds mention in the Atharvaveda and is extolled as "built by gods and prosperous as paradise itself". For millennia, this city on the Sarayu river has been a beacon of dharma, culture, and spiritual seeking.

Ikshvaku dynasty kings ruled here in unbroken succession, and it was in Ayodhya that King Dasharatha nurtured his son Rāma, whose story became the Ramayana—the very soul of Indian ethos.

· rāmāyaṇa ·

The life of Śrī Rāma

The Ramayana, composed by Maharishi Valmiki, immortalised Ayodhya as the backdrop of an epic journey of virtue, love, and duty. From the swayamvara to the exile, the city's very soil is steeped in the memories of Rāma, Sītā, Lakṣmaṇa, and Hanumān.

Historically, the city has been a symbol of righteousness, influencing art and literature across Asia, from Indonesia to Cambodia.

"तदिदं मनुना पूर्वं धर्मसेतुं समाहितम् । अयोध्यायां निवसता सर्वलोकहिताय वै"
— Vālmīki Rāmāyaṇa

Continuum of civilisation

In the 21st century, Ayodhya is witnessing a profound renaissance. The majestic Rāma Mandir rises as a symbol of devotion and architectural splendour. Pilgrims walk its lanes as the city marries ancient wisdom with modernity.

Ayodhya is not just a place; it is a continuity, an idea of return to one's roots.
